## Meet the Fighters

### The Jaguar

The jaguar, scientifically known as *Panthera onca*, is a big cat found primarily in the rainforests, swamps, and grasslands of Central and South America. Known for its muscular build and beautiful rosetted coat, the jaguar is a top predator in its habitat, feared by many and respected by all. With a reputation for being both stealthy and powerful, this feline is a fierce competitor!

**Physical Stats:**
- **Weight:** Up to 250 pounds
- **Length / Height:** Up to 6 feet long
- **Top Speed:** Can run up to 50 miles per hour
- **Bite Force / Strength:** 1,500 PSI

**Weapons:**
- Powerful jaws with a bite force of 1,500 PSI
- Sharp claws
- Stealthy approach

**Fighting Style:**
Jaguars are solitary hunters, often relying on their incredible stealth to ambush prey. They use their powerful jaws to deliver a lethal bite, often targeting the skull or shell of their victims. Quick reflexes and the ability to camouflage in their surroundings give jaguars a significant advantage in hunting and fighting.

### The Griffin

The griffin is a legendary creature with the body of a lion and the head and wings of an eagle. This mythological beast typically resides in mountainous regions and ancient ruins, where it has long been celebrated as a symbol of strength and courage. As a guardian of treasures, the griffin is a creature of both beauty and ferocity.

**Physical Stats:**
- **Weight:** (approx.) 300 pounds
- **Length / Height:** About 13 feet long and 6 feet tall at the shoulder
- **Top Speed:** Can fly up to 30 miles per hour
- **Bite Force / Strength:** (approx.) 1,200 PSI

**Weapons:**
- Sharp claws (up to 6 inches long)
- Powerful beak
- Talons for gripping

**Fighting Style:**
Griffins are known for their aerial prowess, using their strong wings to launch surprise attacks from above. They can swoop down with incredible speed, using their sharp claws and beak to inflict damage. With their keen eyesight, griffins can spot prey from great distances, making them formidable foes in combat.
